The winning message is not “leave AWS/Azure now”; it is “regain control of critical workloads, AI systems, and continuity risk without giving up optionality.” citeturn16view0turn29view0

## Market reality

The market backdrop is straightforward: hyperscaler concentration is still the default state of cloud. Synergy says AWS, Microsoft and Google together accounted for 63% of enterprise spending on cloud infrastructure services in Q3 2025. In the UK, Ofcom found AWS and Microsoft together held 70% to 80% of the cloud infrastructure market in 2022 and the CMA later published provisional and final findings identifying competition concerns in public cloud infrastructure. That concentration is one reason sovereignty language now resonates: buyer unease is not imagined, and regulators are watching the same dependency pattern buyers feel. citeturn4view0turn1search1turn3view4

Regulation is no longer just a compliance sidebar; it is shaping infrastructure architecture itself. The Data Act applies from 12 September 2025 and explicitly aims to help customers transfer data and switch between cloud providers. NIS2 guidance for cloud and data-centre providers emphasises incident handling, business continuity, crisis management and supply-chain security. DORA entered into application on 17 January 2025 and introduces oversight of critical ICT third-party providers because of concentration risk in the financial sector. Schrems II and EDPB guidance continue to force scrutiny of third-country access and supplementary measures for international transfers. The result is that “control,” “continuity,” and “jurisdiction” have become infrastructure design requirements, not just legal review topics. citeturn3view0turn3view2turn0search2turn31search4turn3view3

AI is making the sovereignty question sharper, not softer. The EU’s AI approach combines trust and industrial capacity; the AI Act is now the bloc’s governing framework for trustworthy AI, and the Commission says 19 AI factories across 16 Member States are underway, with a €20 billion facility aimed at up to five AI gigafactories. That means “sovereign AI” is no longer rhetoric alone: Europe is funding compute, governance and industrial capacity in parallel. A landing page aimed at enterprises and governments should therefore connect infrastructure independence to **secure AI deployment, data control and governance-ready operations**. citeturn18search1turn18search6turn28view2turn28view3turn9search11

The vendor market has reacted accordingly, but in fragmented ways. AWS now markets an “independent cloud for Europe” with EU-only infrastructure, EU governance and zero operational access outside EU borders. Microsoft has moved from EU Data Boundary to a broader Sovereign Cloud continuum spanning public and private environments, including measures for disconnected operations and legally binding continuity commitments for European governments. Google offers a portfolio ranging from broader sovereignty controls to fully air-gapped distributed cloud for isolated, sovereign deployments. Oracle emphasises EU legal entities, sovereign realm isolation and even markets protection from CLOUD Act exposure. OVHcloud anchors its proposition on openness, reversibility, transparency and freedom from extraterritorial interference. The common thread is revealing: the market has moved beyond “local data centre” narratives toward law, operations, continuity and control. citeturn10view2turn27view0turn10view1turn10view6turn10view4turn24search3turn10view5

At the same time, private and hybrid infrastructure have regained strategic weight. VMware Cloud Foundation is now explicitly positioned as an AI-ready private-cloud platform combining public-cloud agility with private-cloud security and resilience. Nutanix is framing digital sovereignty as a global imperative tied to hybrid multicloud choice and control. Red Hat OpenShift continues to position edge as a natural extension of hybrid cloud and OpenShift AI as the platform for MLOps, GenAIOps and AgentOps across hybrid environments. IBM’s cloud-transformation research says hybrid cloud has become the dominant architecture, while 71% of surveyed leaders say digital transformation is difficult without a solid hybrid-cloud strategy. This is exactly why GreenPow should present itself as an architecture for **workload placement and control across environments**, not as a single destination. citeturn8search7turn10view8turn10view9turn10view10turn29view0

What the market still lacks is a standard definition buyers can trust. The Commission had to create a framework that measures sovereignty across strategic, legal, operational, environmental, supply-chain, openness, security and compliance dimensions, with SEAL levels ranging from absence of sovereignty to full EU supply chain. The same Commission says this tender moved the market away from abstract principles to concrete sovereignty metrics. Gaia-X, meanwhile, says plainly that it is not a cloud platform or product, but a federation and standards effort. This means GreenPow’s page must make its own definition of sovereignty concrete, testable and operational. citeturn17view0turn28view0

## Buyer priorities

The buyers most likely to convert are not hunting for “another cloud vendor.” They are trying to reduce specific kinds of risk while still supporting modern workloads, especially AI.

**CIO / digital and infrastructure executive.** Their goal is to reduce strategic dependency, improve negotiating leverage with hyperscalers, make costs more predictable and ensure that critical operations are not exposed to one vendor’s roadmap, outage model or legal perimeter. Procurement triggers include cloud-cost volatility, board-level questions about geopolitical exposure, and a need to modernise legacy environments without deepening lock-in. Those priorities map directly to concentration concerns, Data Act switching provisions, FinOps expansion beyond public cloud, and the re-emergence of hybrid cloud as the dominant operating model. citeturn3view4turn3view0turn29view1turn29view2turn29view0

**CTO / platform engineering / infrastructure leadership.** This buyer wants a consistent operating model across private, sovereign, hybrid and edge infrastructure; workload portability; support for containers, VMs and AI pipelines; and an automation layer that reduces estate fragmentation. Their fear is not just lock-in, but operational sprawl: too many tools, too many environments, and too little policy consistency. The evidence base here is strong: IBM highlights siloed cloud operations and skills shortages; Red Hat emphasises operational consistency from core to edge; and Nutanix and VMware now both centre choice, control and AI-ready hybrid infrastructure. citeturn29view0turn10view9turn7search1turn8search7

**CISO / risk / security leadership.** Their priorities are preventing extra-territorial or unauthorised access, proving effective controls over sensitive workloads, supporting incident response and continuity, and enabling secure AI in regulated environments. They care about confidential computing, attestation, encryption-key custody, operator access controls, supply-chain resilience and auditable boundaries. NIS2, DORA, EDPB guidance and the growth of confidential-computing adoption all reinforce that security buyers now need evidence across data in transit, at rest and in use. citeturn3view2turn31search4turn3view3turn12search8turn11search1turn11search2turn11search11

**Public sector, defence-adjacent and critical-infrastructure operators.** These buyers are driven by continuity under crisis conditions, legal jurisdiction, supplier assurance, and the ability to run sensitive or disconnected workloads. They respond to language around national capability, operational survivability, and independently supportable systems far more than generic elasticity or developer convenience. That posture is visible in the Commission’s sovereign-cloud procurement, in ANSSI/BSI work on sovereignty criteria, in Google’s air-gapped sovereign deployments, and in sovereign offerings explicitly aimed at public sector and regulated sectors. citeturn17view0turn24search1turn10view6turn10view2

**Regulated industries such as financial services, healthcare, energy and public administration.** Their buying threshold is crossed when sovereignty, resilience and compliance converge. DORA forces financial entities to manage ICT dependency and withstand disruptions; NIS2 pulls digital infrastructure, cloud providers and critical sectors into stricter cyber and continuity expectations; and healthcare and public-sector examples are recurring in sovereign-cloud case material. For these buyers, the landing page should show that GreenPow is designed for workloads that are *too sensitive to be left to generic policy controls alone*. citeturn31search4turn0search2turn3view2turn24search2turn10view2

The highest-value use cases cluster around five patterns. First, **sovereign AI** for copilots, analytics and inference on regulated data, where AI governance, locality and secure model operations matter as much as raw GPU access. Second, **regulated application estates** that need private-plus-public workload placement under one control model. Third, **critical systems and edge operations** where low latency, survivability or disconnection matters. Fourth, **resilience and cyber recovery** for workloads that need defined recovery objectives, isolation and failover control. Fifth, **confidential workloads and secure data collaboration**, where data-in-use protection becomes a prerequisite for AI and cross-organisation analysis. These patterns are visible across the EU’s AI-factory strategy, Google Distributed Cloud, Red Hat’s edge and AI positioning, IBM’s financial-services cloud, and the industry-wide rise of confidential computing. citeturn28view2turn10view6turn10view9turn10view10turn10view7turn12search8

## Competitive positioning

The main competitive mistake would be to fight hyperscalers at their own game. AWS, Microsoft, Google and Oracle now all have sovereign-cloud stories, and those stories are getting stronger. AWS is stressing independent EU operation, local governance and validated sovereignty assurances. Microsoft is combining public-cloud sovereignty, private-cloud sovereignty, EU data boundary, continuity commitments and European governance. Google is spanning partner-led sovereignty, assured workloads and fully air-gapped distributed deployments. Oracle is stressing EU entities, sovereign realms and contractual/legal safeguards. In other words: **“we host in Europe” is no longer a differentiator**. citeturn10view2turn27view0turn10view1turn10view3turn10view6turn10view4

European sovereign-cloud providers occupy a different space. OVHcloud leads with reversibility, transparency and freedom from extraterritorial interference. STACKIT describes itself as sovereign and GDPR-compliant with a European technology stack. Scaleway now openly frames itself as a sovereign public cloud and AI platform for EU institutions. The Commission’s 2026 procurement result is especially important because it awarded four contracts, mostly at SEAL-3 “digital resilience” level, and explicitly said EU providers are closing the gap while delivering strong managed-service capability. That validates a real market opening for European-first control and resilience positions. citeturn10view5turn22search2turn22search1turn17view0

Private-cloud and hybrid-platform vendors are a third comparison set. VMware, Nutanix, Red Hat and IBM are not, by themselves, legal answers to digital sovereignty; they are operational answers to control, portability, consistency and modernisation. They give buyers a way to run VMs, containers, AI and edge workloads across estates. That matters for GreenPow because it means the practical alternative to hyperscalers is often not “a sovereign cloud,” but “a sovereign operating model on top of hybrid infrastructure.” That is much closer to GreenPow’s opportunity space. citeturn8search7turn10view8turn10view9turn10view10turn10view7

GreenPow’s clearest opening is therefore a more opinionated proposition:

- **Not** a commodity host.  
- **Not** a pure public-cloud alternative.  
- **Not** an ideological repatriation message.  
- **But** a platform for operating critical workloads and AI with jurisdictional control, resilience engineering, workload portability, and distributed execution across private, sovereign, hybrid and edge environments.

That positioning is where current vendor messaging is still thin. Most competitors major on locality, jurisdiction or service breadth. Fewer lead with **survivability**, **degraded-mode continuity**, **workload-level control**, **operator trust boundaries**, and **hybrid AI orchestration** all at once. citeturn16view0turn17view0turn10view6turn27view0

The principal positioning risks are equally clear. If GreenPow says “sovereign cloud” without defining it, buyers will project their own meaning onto the term and confusion will spike. If it says “private cloud” too loudly, it risks sounding like legacy infrastructure. If it says “edge” too early, it may sound niche or industrial-only. If it leads with sustainability, it risks sounding secondary to the board-level issues already driving infrastructure decisions. The page should therefore anchor on **control, resilience and independence**, then explain that this is delivered through private, sovereign, hybrid and edge architecture. **Objection handling**

**“Why not AWS or Azure?”**  
Use them where they fit. But if your critical workloads require tighter jurisdictional control, clearer operator boundaries, workload portability and continuity that is not dependent on a single global control plane, you need an infrastructure model that is broader than one hyperscaler.

**“This sounds expensive.”**  
Dependency already has a cost: switching friction, duplicated controls, AI-spend opacity, overprovisioning and resilience gaps. The right comparison is not against the cheapest VM; it is against the cost of concentrated operational risk and unpredictable spend.

**“Do we really need sovereign cloud?”**  
Not for every workload. Yes for the systems where extra-territorial access risk, regulatory pressure, continuity requirements, or AI-data sensitivity make generic controls insufficient. The buying motion should be workload-scoped, not absolutist.

**“Can this scale globally?”**  
The point is not to rebuild a hyperscaler footprint from scratch. The point is to place the right workloads in the right control domains, while keeping architecture and operations consistent across private, sovereign, hybrid and edge environments.

**“How do you ensure resilience?”**  
By designing resilience into topology, operations and recovery: independent failure domains, tested failover, workload placement rules, degraded-mode operations and automation-led recovery.

**“Can this support AI workloads?”**  
Yes — if AI is treated as an infrastructure-governance problem as well as a model problem. The page should show governed AI deployment, confidential-workload controls, data-locality boundaries and orchestration across environments.

**“What about compliance?”**  
Do not claim “full compliance” in the abstract. Instead show how GreenPow supports compliance obligations through control points, evidence, documentation, locality choices, access restrictions and auditability.

- Reference architecture diagram for private, sovereign, hybrid and edge deployment

- Resilience model showing failure domains, RTO/RPO targets, failover and degraded mode

- Benchmark evidence for deployment speed, failover testing and AI workload economics  
- Relevant certifications and attestations, shown with scope and boundaries rather than logo dumping

## GTM recommendations

The near-term market opportunity is strongest in **European public sector, financial services, healthcare, energy, industrial operators and other critical-infrastructure environments** where sovereignty, resilience and AI governance are already procurement-level concerns. That conclusion is supported by the Commission’s sovereign-cloud procurement, DORA’s resilience regime for financial entities, NIS2’s scope expansion and Europe’s AI-factory investment strategy. citeturn17view0turn31search4turn3view2turn28view2turn28view3

For GTM, GreenPow should lead with a **workload-led offer**, not a platform-led one. The first conversion motion should be something like: *Assess your critical workloads for sovereignty, resilience and AI-governance risk.* That is easier to buy than “move to our platform,” and it mirrors how the market is actually behaving: selective control and workload placement, not wholesale abandonment of global providers. citeturn16view0turn29view0

The recommended GTM moves are these:

- Focus initial campaigns on **regulated AI**, **critical operations continuity**, and **hyperscaler dependency reduction** rather than broad cloud transformation.
- Segment by risk posture: public sector / regulated enterprise / critical operator / AI-sensitive enterprise.
- Publish one serious technical proof asset before scaling paid demand: the reference architecture or sovereignty-control model.
- Pair every messaging claim with a proof mechanism: architecture, control, test result, or compliance mapping.
- Avoid absolutist anti-hyperscaler positioning; sell **control over placement**, not ideological replacement.
- Treat sustainability as a supporting differentiator tied to efficient workload placement, infrastructure discipline and reduced waste — not as the lead story. citeturn17view0turn29view1turn29view2
