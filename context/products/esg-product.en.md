# GreenPow Positioning for the ESG and Sustainability Market

## Executive summary

The strongest sustainability opportunity for GreenPow is **not** to sell itself as another ESG software vendor and **not** to market itself as “green hosting.” The market evidence points in a different direction: enterprises care about sustainability, decarbonisation, and CSRD-readiness, but they buy technology when it helps them produce **reporting-grade evidence**, improve **operational efficiency**, reduce **energy and infrastructure waste**, and maintain **control** over critical workloads in hybrid or sovereign environments. CSRD still matters, even though the European Commission has proposed to narrow the scope to the largest companies; in parallel, ESRS E1 still anchors disclosure around energy consumption and mix plus gross Scopes 1, 2, and 3, and CSRD reporting remains assurance-bound and digitally tagged. ## What enterprise sustainability buyers actually want

### Why CSRD still matters

From a demand perspective, CSRD is still a live commercial driver, but it is no longer wise to frame it as a one-dimensional “massive mandatory market.” The European Commission states that the first wave of companies already had to apply the new rules for the 2024 financial year, reported in 2025, and that reporting is built around ESRS. At the same time, the Commission’s current package proposes narrowing CSRD to companies with more than 1,000 employees, which means direct compliance urgency is more concentrated than many vendors implied in 2023–2024. That does **not** kill demand; it changes where urgency sits. Large companies still need reporting-grade systems, and smaller firms still face supply-chain pressure, customer questionnaires, lender scrutiny, and procurement requirements shaped by those larger firms. citeturn23view2turn18view3turn35view0

ESRS E1 makes infrastructure materially relevant. The standard explicitly requires disclosure on **energy consumption and mix** and on **gross Scope 1, Scope 2, Scope 3, and total GHG emissions** when climate is material. Deloitte’s CSRD guidance also highlights that sustainability information must be assured and digitally tagged, which immediately raises the bar for traceability, data control, and evidence quality. In practice, this means that infrastructure is no longer just “back office IT”; it becomes part of the evidence chain behind environmental reporting and assurance. citeturn28view3turn28view1turn34view0

The implementation pain is real. Datamaran’s 2025 CSRD pulse check found that the biggest challenges were value-chain integration, data gaps, assurance readiness, and the double materiality process. Auditors and supervisory bodies were expected to be the most engaged audiences for CSRD reports. KPMG likewise notes that many companies are adopting CSRD-style practices before they are strictly required. In other words, buyers do not need another abstract sustainability narrative; they need systems that reduce the operational burden of collecting, structuring, validating, and evidencing data. citeturn19view0turn18view3turn18view4

### How sustainable infrastructure is being evaluated

The evidence suggests that enterprise buyers evaluate sustainable infrastructure through a pragmatic blend of **efficiency**, **governance**, **reporting integrity**, and **risk control**. Deloitte’s 2025 C-suite Sustainability Report says sustainability remains a top-three C-suite priority, alongside technology adoption and AI, and that revenue generation was the most frequently reported benefit of sustainability actions. PwC’s 2025 State of Decarbonization similarly argues that companies are still investing, often more quietly, because they see sustainability as tied to resilience, energy demand, customer expectations, and long-term growth. Workiva’s 2025 benchmark adds that 97% of executives believe strong sustainability reporting will be a competitive advantage in the next two years, while 96% of institutional investors say integrated reporting enables better decision-making. citeturn25view2turn25view3turn35view0turn18view0turn18view1

For infrastructure specifically, buyers want three things at once. First, they want **granular measurement**: by site, region, service, workload, time period, and business unit. Second, they want **normalised decision-making**, where cost and carbon can be evaluated together rather than in separate reporting silos. Third, they want **operational realism**: sustainability improvements that do not compromise latency, resilience, privacy, or compliance. The FinOps Foundation now defines sustainability as decision-making that balances environmental value with financial value across cloud, data centres, data platforms, and AI, and explicitly says organisations need documented assumptions, estimation methodologies, and data-quality limits to maintain reporting integrity and reduce regulatory exposure. citeturn24view0turn24view1turn24view2

That aligns with current infrastructure purchasing behaviour. Uptime Institute’s 2025 survey shows enterprises are still hybrid by design: 45% of IT workloads remain in enterprise data centres, and few large organisations are comfortable moving everything out of their own facilities because sovereignty and regulatory concerns remain top of mind. The same survey shows that sustainability reporting progress has stalled, average PUE is still hovering around 1.54, and buyers remain constrained by legacy assets, power availability, and operational trade-offs. Gartner adds another important signal: 64% of executive leaders say they do not get the emissions performance data they need from vendors, which directly limits prioritisation and adoption of sustainable IT initiatives. citeturn26view2turn26view1turn26view0turn24view4turn24view5

## How GreenPow should position itself

### The category to claim

The most credible category for GreenPow is:

**Auditable sustainable infrastructure for regulated enterprises**

A more differentiated variant, if GreenPow truly supports dynamic placement and policy-based optimisation, is:

**Carbon-aware sovereign infrastructure for hybrid estates**

Both are stronger than “green cloud,” “green hosting,” or “ESG software.” They keep GreenPow high enough in the stack to protect margin and differentiation, but still close enough to buyer pain to be understandable. They also preserve GreenPow’s existing foundations in private cloud, sovereign and hybrid environments, edge and micro data centres, privacy-first design, resilience, and automated optimisation.

The positioning statement should sound like this:

> GreenPow helps regulated enterprises measure, optimise, and evidence the sustainability performance of their infrastructure across private cloud, on-premise, edge, and selected public cloud environments—so energy, carbon, resilience, and compliance can be managed together, not in separate silos.

This framing is more durable because it matches what the market already rewards: visibility, control, efficiency, and reporting integrity. It also aligns with emerging EU and global infrastructure realities, where sovereignty, supply-chain resilience, data locality, and auditability increasingly sit beside sustainability rather than apart from it. citeturn32view0turn23view3turn24view1turn26view2

### How to explain carbon-aware infrastructure

GreenPow should explain carbon-aware infrastructure in operational, not ideological, terms:

> **Carbon-aware infrastructure means using energy, carbon, and workload data to decide where, when, and how workloads should run—without breaking resilience, latency, privacy, or compliance requirements.**

That explanation is defensible because it mirrors how the market is defining the space. Google shows that flexible workloads can be shifted across time and location to use cleaner grids, while explicitly noting that privacy and other limits still apply. Microsoft and the Green Software Foundation describe carbon-aware computing as shifting compute to times and places with lower carbon intensity, supported by common standards and open tooling. The FinOps Foundation now treats sustainability as a normal infrastructure optimisation decision alongside cost and operational value. citeturn29view2turn29view3turn20view2turn20view3turn24view1

That said, GreenPow should **avoid overselling** carbon-aware claims. Carbon-aware optimisation is strongest for flexible or batch-style workloads. It is less useful for latency-sensitive, always-on, or legally constrained workloads. Google’s own examples make that limitation clear. So GreenPow should position carbon-aware infrastructure as a **policy-governed optimisation capability**, not a miracle feature that makes every workload sustainable. citeturn29view2

### What to lead with and what to avoid

GreenPow should lead with a three-part value architecture:

| Layer | What GreenPow should say | Why it works |
|---|---|---|
| Operational control | “Measure and optimise energy, carbon, and utilisation across your hybrid estate.” | Speaks to infrastructure teams, FinOps, and procurement |
| Reporting integrity | “Create reporting-grade evidence for CSRD, Scope 2 management, internal carbon governance, and audits.” | Speaks to sustainability, finance, and assurance stakeholders |
| Strategic trust | “Deploy in private, sovereign, or hybrid environments with clear data boundaries and enterprise control.” | Speaks to regulated buyers and differentiates from generic cloud narratives |

The company should **not** lead with these patterns:

| Pattern to avoid | Why it is weak |
|---|---|
| “Green hosting” | Sounds SMB-level, commoditised, and too narrow for enterprise hybrid estates |
| “Save the planet with cloud” | Feels activist and non-commercial; easy to distrust |
| “Net zero infrastructure” without methodology | Creates immediate substantiation risk |
| “AI-powered sustainability” as the main story | Pushes GreenPow into a noisier and less trusted category before its proof is established |
| “Carbon neutral” or “eco-friendly” shorthand | High greenwashing risk unless rigorously defined and evidenced |

An important nuance is **Scope 2**. GreenPow should be precise here. If a customer owns or operates private infrastructure, electricity use and energy mix can feed directly into Scope 2 and ESRS E1 reporting. If a customer primarily consumes public cloud, the underlying cloud emissions usually sit in supplier-value-chain accounting, and buyers increasingly rely on provider tools that report usage-associated emissions across all three scopes. In other words, GreenPow should not claim to “solve Scope 2” for every deployment model; it should say that it improves **energy and carbon measurement for owned and hybrid infrastructure** and supports broader reporting through auditable exports and integrations. That honesty will increase enterprise trust. citeturn23view0turn21view3turn21view4turn21view5turn15search3

## Strategic recommendations and GTM implications

GreenPow should enter this market from the **infrastructure evidence gap**, not from the broad ESG software category. The best wedge is the enterprise buyer who already has sustainability obligations or pressure, already has a hybrid estate, and already feels the pain of fragmented measurements across owned and purchased infrastructure. That makes the most promising initial markets **European regulated organisations, industrial and manufacturing groups, energy and utility operators, complex services businesses with private or hybrid infrastructure, and public-sector-adjacent organisations with sovereignty needs**. This is partly a market-read inference, but it is supported by the EU’s growing focus on cloud sovereignty, data-centre energy reporting, and hybrid reality in enterprise estates. citeturn32view0turn23view3turn26view2

The GTM motion should start with a concrete commercial promise such as **“infrastructure sustainability baseline in 30 days”** or **“hybrid energy and carbon evidence pack for CSRD readiness”** rather than a broad transformation narrative. Sustainability leaders buy confidence, but CIOs and CFOs buy controllable deliverables. A short assessment-led motion fits both. It also creates the raw material for case studies, benchmark data, and a stronger category story later. This approach is consistent with the way competitors build trust: Watershed highlights methodology and lineage, Sweep emphasises a single trusted source and audit readiness, Google and AWS emphasise exportable, provider-specific emissions data, and HPE/IBM emphasise hybrid visibility tied to cost and operational decisions. citeturn22view5turn22view4turn21view3turn21view4turn20view7turn22view1

GreenPow should also avoid competing head-on with mature reporting platforms in phase one. A better strategy is to become the **sustainability infrastructure layer that feeds them**. In practice, that means integrating with ESG platforms, BI tools, or assurance workflows instead of rebuilding the front-end reporting stack immediately. The market already contains strong reporting vendors; the weaker link is often the infrastructure-side evidence chain and the ability to connect technical telemetry to trustworthy, normalised outputs. citeturn22view4turn22view5turn22view6turn22view7turn24view1

A final strategic caution: do not let “sustainable AI infrastructure” overshadow the core positioning too early. The energy scrutiny around AI and data centres is rising quickly, and serious institutions are calling for more resource-use transparency. That makes AI a useful supporting angle, but a risky headline if GreenPow cannot yet provide unusually strong proof. Lead with **sustainable infrastructure and carbon-aware operations**; treat AI as one workload class inside that story, not the story itself. citeturn23view6turn24view3turn29view3

### Open questions and limitations

The largest unresolved variable is **GreenPow’s current product maturity**. Public market research can show what buyers want and where the whitespace sits, but it cannot verify whether GreenPow today can genuinely deliver workload-level energy/carbon normalisation, cross-provider reconciliation, sovereign deployment options, facility-to-workload traceability, or standards-aligned exports at enterprise grade.

Before launching a large demand-generation push, GreenPow should answer these internal questions clearly:

| Open question | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| What data is measured directly versus estimated? | Determines trust, claim scope, and assurance readiness |
| Can GreenPow reconcile private, on-premise, edge, and public cloud data into one carbon ledger? | This is central to the proposed differentiation |
| What deployment models can be demonstrated today? | Without proof of private/hybrid/sovereign deployment, the sovereignty story weakens |
| Can GreenPow map outputs to ESRS E1, Scope 2 workflows, and adjacent data-centre KPIs? | This determines compliance relevance |
| Does GreenPow have measured customer outcomes yet? | Without this, the market will treat it as a concept rather than a platform |
| What standards or third-party validations can be referenced now? | Necessary to defend sustainability claims and avoid greenwashing risk |

If GreenPow can answer those questions with evidence, its sustainability positioning can be strong. If it cannot, the right move is not broader branding; it is a narrower first offering with tighter claims, stronger proof, and a sharper wedge.
